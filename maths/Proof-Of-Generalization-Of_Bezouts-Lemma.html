Proof of the Generalization of Bezout's Lemma

Introduction

Bazout's Lemma state that, for every integers \(a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\) there exist \(x, y \in \mathbb{Z}\) such that: \[ ax + by = \gcd(a, b) \] Moreover, every number of the form \(ax + by\) is a multiple of \(\gcd(a, b)\).

We are going to prove the generalization of the Lemma, which states that, for every set of \(s\) integers \(m_1,\ m_2,\ ...,\ m_s\) there exist coefficients \(x_1,\ ...,\ x_s\), such that: \[ m_1x_1 + m_2x_2 + ... + m_sx_s = \gcd(m_1, m_2, ..., m_s) \] From now on we are going to symbolize the \(\gcd(a_1, ..., a_n)\) simplily with parethesis \((a_1, ..., a_n)\).

The Special case first

Let's first prove the Lemma for two non-negative integers. Let \(a, b \in \mathbb{N_0}\). By (strong) induction. We are going to apply induction in terms of the biggest of the two numbers. Without loss of generailty we can suppose that \(a \geq b\).

Base case: For \(a = 1\) we can easily check for both values of \(b = 0, 1\) that Bezout's lemma holds.

Induction step: As noted above, we are going to use strong induction. The induction hypothesis is the following:

Suppose that for every \(0 < p \leq a\) and for every \(0 < q < p\) there exist coefficients \(x, y \in \mathbb{Z}\) such that: \[ px + qy = \gcd(p, q) \]
We are going to now proove that for every pair \((a + 1, b)\) where \(b < a + 1\) Bezout's lemma is also true. Apply the euclidean division \(a + 1 : b\) and so: \[ a + 1 = kb + r \qquad (1) \] where \(0 \leq r < b\). We know that \(\gcd(a + 1, b) = \gcd(b, r)\). But \(r < b\) and \(b \leq a\). From the induction hypothesis there exist coefficients \(x, y \in \mathbb{Z}\) such that: \[ bx + ry = \gcd(b, r) \] But, from (1), we have \(r = a + 1 - kb\) and \(\gcd(b, r) = \gcd(a + 1, b)\). And so: \[ bv + (a + 1 - kb)u = \gcd(a + 1, b) \] And thus: \[ b(v - ku) + (a + 1)k = \gcd(a + 1, b) \] And thus Bezout's lemma is also true for every pair \((a + 1, b)\) with \(b < a + 1\).

To proove Bezout's lemma for all integers, not just non-negative, it is a matter or finding the Bezout coefficients for the absolute values of \(a, b\) and then switching the signes of those coefficients if necessary.
